ccp_alphas = [0.97, 0.35, 0.125, 0.08, 0.045, 0.025, 0.01]

clfs = []
train_scores = []
test_scores = []

for alpha in ccp_alphas:
    clf = tree.DecisionTreeClassifier(random_state=RANDOM_STATE, ccp_alpha=alpha)
    clf.fit(X_train, y_train)
    clfs.append(clf)
    train_scores.append(clf.score(X_train, y_train))
    test_scores.append(clf.score(X_test, y_test))

Task 9

As ccp_alpha increases, model complexity decreases. Larger values enforce stronger pruning, producing very simple trees like Models 13 with depth 0 and a single leaf node. Smaller values reduce pruning, allowing deeper trees with more leaf nodes and better capturing patterns in the training data.

Model 7, with ccp_alpha = 0.010, achieved the highest accuracy on both training and testing sets at 0.86. It had a depth of 4 and 7 leaf nodes, making it the most complex model. Its consistent performance across both datasets indicates strong generalization. In contrast, Models 13 underfit the data, predicting only the majority class and achieving 0.50 accuracy on both sets.

Accuracy was the primary metric because overall classification performance was the focus. Model 5 achieved 0.85 accuracy with only five leaf nodes and a depth of 3, offering nearly identical performance to Model 7 but with lower complexity. For interpretability and presentation, Model 5 would be preferred due to its simpler structure and comparable predictive strength.

Note: In real-world applications, the variable duration should be excluded from predictive modeling since it is only known after the marketing calls. However, the models in this assignment could still inform operational rules, such as setting a cutoff to wrap up promotional calls exceeding a certain length.
